Vintage sockets and lightswitches

The design and styling of sockets and lightswitch types in Italy has varied considerably throughout the years. Indeed, while in other countries generally their styling hasn't changed very much, in Italy much more attention has been paid to their design, which has thus changed quite a bit over time according to the style of the period.

Sockets, lightswitches and various other devices meant to go in a wall box are generally known as "series". Various series have been released over the years by various brands, each with a different appearance.
A trendsetter in this field was definitely BTicino, whose styling influenced and was cloned by many other brands, as you'll see shortly.

Surface-mount items

The earliest types of sockets and lightswitches were surface-mount: meant to be screwed directly onto the wall. This makes sense, as electricity was still in its early days back then and all houses would have had to be retrofitted.

While this style of mounting fell out of fashion in favour of wallboxes, especially as houses started being built with electrical installations in mind, they were and still are available for purchase. Surface mount items are more of an eyesore, but they make up for it, in certain cases, for their flexibility and ease of installation.

Vimar 10A socket

Rating: likely 10A 250V

This is a later style of surface mount socket, made by the company Vimar. It's built very simply: the body is made of plastic and there's a ceramic section on the back that holds the metal contacts in place. Holes have been cut out in the plastic to make space for the wires.

Devices for round boxes

The round box was the earliest type of wall box in use in Italy (prior to that wall-mounted items were used instead). This style of box is still used in other European countries, however in Italy it was superseded by rectangular boxes.

The way devices mount to this style of box is quite interesting, and different from modern ones: the box itself doesn't have any screw holes, instead the item is held with arms that expand outwards and grab onto the sides of the box.

Early electrical devices

Early devices meant for wallboxes were often a more premium alternative to ones that mounted externally to the wall, which was at the time much more common (and presumably easier to retrofit).

Compared to other models shown later, whose styling was designed to be visible and look good inside a room, these early examples instead did the opposite, trying their best to be hidden from sight. As such, they were often made with a clear (glass, and later plastic) front piece, to be easier to conceal behind a wallpaper, with only the bare miniumum being visible.

Early three-phase 15A socket

Rating: likely 15A 380V

This is an excellent example of how these early styles of sockets used to work. As indicated by the front informative cardboard piece (which likely means that this socket was never actually installed), it's made by the company Saigir (?) from Trieste, and it's a three-phase socket with earth connection.

The way this socket is meant to be installed is quite interesting, and totally different from the later styles which just had screws on the front or behind a faceplate: the three holes where the pins of the plug go into protrude a little, and can be easily twisted off.
After having removed them the front clear plastic piece comes off, revealing the mounting screws. Wiring can be done on the back, using the four (3 phases + earth) terminals which are housed in a ceramic piece.

Dual lightswitch, unknown brand

Rating: 6A 250V

This double lightswitch follows the same general style of installation as the socket shown earlier, although the front cover plate is sadly missing. It's not clear who actually built this socket, although the distinctive logos present on the back suggest that it might perhaps also have been built by the same company, or by another one based in Trieste.

The lightswitches themselves are quite interesting as they're a style that's not used over here anymore (although it's quite common in North America) - the switch has a plastic lever on the front which sticks out and is moved up or down, similar to toggleswitches used in electronic items.

The BTicino Domino series

Released in the 50s, the Domino series was hugely popular and used in a lot of houses built, or retrofitted to have electricity, during the decade.
Their design was quite interesting, as they were generally made out of a single moulded piece of bakelite/plastic, which (in the case of the sockets) formed both the body of the socket itself and the thing that screwed into the wall.

On the back there are, as expected, the wiring connections. They are formed of screw terminals (thus, capable of also accepting stranded wire) plainly exposed on the back and very easy to touch once the socket's removed from the wall.

Other later round box devices

While Italy switched in the 60s-70s to the rectangular box standard, items for round boxes were and still continue to be made, primarily for replacements for older houses but there's nothing preventing someone from using round boxes even nowadays.

So, even though the Magic series (which you're about to see) did introduce the concept of rectangular boxes, it was still quite common for people to use them with round boxes like they did before.

Devices for rectangular boxes

BTicino Magic modular devices

The Magic series introduced the idea of modular systems. It was incredibly popular, so much that you can still buy replacement parts for it nowadays, and several clones were made.

The modular design meant that devices now weren't made of a single block, insted being divided into modules, a support plate, and a cover plate. This style of design has been hugely influential and is the way every socket or lightswitch is made in Italy nowadays.

Other types of sockets

"Marbo" fused multi-socket device

Rating: 15A 380V

This device accepts various types of plug: it has a small type L socket, a big type L socket, and two three-phase ones. It would have likely been useful in a workshop or factory where various types of equipment may have been connected.
The sockets have a maximum wattage rating written next to them: 500W for the small type L one, 800W for the large one, and 1000W for each three-phase socket.

The device also has a main on/off switch (with a label instructing the user to switch the power off before removing a plug, something I'm sure nobody ever actually did), and fuses - the idea probably was that you'd have a bunch of these sockets all connected to a single high amperage circuit, and then the sockets can are fused down individually.

It's interesting to note that the small type L socket is labelled as for "lighting", and it has separate terminal connections, which also means that it wouldn't have been conencted to the fuses.
I assume this might have been so that you could easily connect this to a separate lighting circuit for the building (with a smaller fuse rating), or maybe for a cheaper lighting supply (I'm not sure if factories of the time would have had such a thing like residential buildings did).
